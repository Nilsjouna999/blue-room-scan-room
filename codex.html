The Western Zodiac

the twelve sun signs

A birth chart places all three inner-life pillars in a sign, and they don't repeat the same meaning. The SUN sign describes core identity — ego, willpower, the basic self one is growing into; it's what people mean by 'my sign.' The MOON sign describes the inner and emotional nature — instinctive reactions, comfort needs, and how a person processes feeling, often operating beneath conscious awareness. The RISING sign (Ascendant) is the outward manner and first impression — the social mask, physical demeanor, and style through which the Sun and Moon get expressed to the world. Two people can share a Sun sign and seem completely different once Moon and Rising are factored in.

The Chinese Zodiac & Five Elements

twelve animals, five elements

The Chinese zodiac assigns one of 12 animals to each lunar year in a fixed 12-year cycle, layered with one of 5 elements (Wu Xing) that each cycle through in a 60-year Stem-Branch combination — so a person's year animal is their public, outward self, while the element is the underlying material it is forged from and colors how that animal's traits are expressed. Chinese New Year falls in late January or February (not January 1), so anyone born before that year's lunar new year date technically belongs to the previous year's animal — a January or early-February birth should always be checked against the exact lunar boundary rather than assumed.

Numerology

the core & master numbers

The same core or master number is read in four different positions of a numerology chart, each answering a different question. LIFE PATH — calculated from the full birth date — describes the person's overall purpose and the main lesson of the lifetime. EXPRESSION (also called DESTINY) — calculated from the full birth name — describes natural talents and abilities the person is here to develop and express. SOUL URGE (also called HEART'S DESIRE) — calculated from the vowels in the birth name — describes inner motivation, what the person deeply wants and values. PERSONAL YEAR — calculated from the birth date combined with the current calendar year — describes the theme and lessons of that specific year in a recurring nine-year cycle.

The Major Arcana

the twenty-two

The 22 Major Arcana trace the Fool's Journey, a symbolic arc of the soul from innocence through trial to wholeness; each card represents a major archetypal force or life lesson rather than a day-to-day event (the domain of the Minor Arcana). In the tarot birth-card (or "Soul Card") system, a person's birth date is reduced to a single number (or two-digit pair) that maps to a Major Arcana card, taken to represent the core lesson or energy governing that person's life; e.g. reducing to 7 points to The Chariot, reducing to 22/4 points to The Emperor (or The Fool, in the 0-21 count), and so on depending on the reduction method used.

The Fool

0 · Major Arcana

new beginningsinnocencespontaneityleap of faithfreedom

The Fool represents new beginnings, innocence, and a leap into the unknown. It depicts a free spirit stepping toward a cliff's edge, trusting in the journey rather than the destination. The card signals openness to experience, spontaneity, and a willingness to take risks without over-planning. It marks the start of the Fool's Journey through the rest of the Major Arcana.

Reversed Reversed, The Fool warns of recklessness, naivety, or poor judgment. It can indicate being unprepared for risk, acting without thinking through consequences, or being held back by fear of taking a needed leap.

The Magician

I · Major Arcana

manifestationwillpowerresourcefulnessskillaction

The Magician stands for manifestation, willpower, and the resourceful use of one's skills and tools to turn ideas into reality. Traditionally shown with the four suit symbols on a table and one hand raised to the sky, one pointing to earth, he channels higher inspiration into concrete action. The card signals confidence, focus, and the ability to make things happen. It represents the point where potential becomes purposeful action.

Reversed Reversed, The Magician suggests manipulation, trickery, or untapped talent. It can point to poor planning, deceit, or someone using their skills for selfish or dishonest ends.

The High Priestess

II · Major Arcana

The High Priestess represents intuition, the subconscious mind, and hidden or as-yet-unrevealed knowledge. Seated between two pillars, veiled from ordinary sight, she guards mysteries that must be accessed through inner reflection rather than logic. The card calls for patience, listening to inner guidance, and trusting what is felt over what is proven. It signals secrets, dreams, and knowledge not yet ready to be spoken.

Reversed Reversed, she suggests disconnection from intuition, secrets coming to light, or information being withheld or misused. It can point to confusion, repressed feelings, or ignoring one's inner voice.

The Empress

III · Major Arcana

abundancenurturingfertilitynaturecreativity

The Empress embodies abundance, nurturing, fertility, and the creative force of nature. She is often shown enthroned amid lush growth, symbolizing motherhood, sensuality, and material and creative flourishing. The card signals a period of growth, comfort, and connection to the natural world or one's own creative and nurturing instincts. It can also relate literally to pregnancy, birth, or a growing project.

Reversed Reversed, The Empress can indicate creative block, neglect (of self or others), smothering or codependent nurturing, or issues around fertility, body image, or overindulgence.

The Emperor

IV · Major Arcana

The Emperor represents authority, structure, and stable, rational leadership. Seated on a stone throne, he embodies order, discipline, and the establishment of rules and boundaries that provide security. The card signals a father-figure energy: protection through structure, strategic thinking, and the will to build and defend something lasting. It favors logic and long-term planning over impulsiveness.

Reversed Reversed, The Emperor points to domination, rigidity, or abuse of power, or conversely a lack of discipline and structure. It can indicate an overbearing authority figure or a loss of control.

The Hierophant represents tradition, established institutions, and conventional belief systems such as religion, education, or formal codes of conduct. The card signals conformity, seeking guidance through proven systems, and rites of passage such as marriage or formal education.

Reversed Reversed, The Hierophant suggests rebellion against convention, unconventional beliefs, or challenging outdated institutions. It can also indicate restriction from rigid dogma or personal, unorthodox spirituality.

Traditionally depicted as a man and woman before an angel, it echoes themes of duality and union. The card signals attraction, partnership, and moments demanding a decision that aligns with one's core values.

Reversed Reversed, The Lovers can indicate disharmony, imbalance in a relationship, or a difficult choice made poorly or avoided. It may point to infidelity, misalignment of values, or self-love issues.

The card signals overcoming obstacles, maintaining control amid conflicting forces, and pushing decisively toward a goal.

Reversed Reversed, The Chariot suggests lack of direction, loss of control, or being pulled in opposing directions. It can indicate aggression, self-doubt, or a failure to assert the will needed to move forward.

Reversed Reversed, Death suggests resistance to change, being stuck in a stagnant situation, or a fear of letting go that delays necessary transformation.

The Sun represents joy, success, and vitality. It depicts a radiant sun shining over a child (often riding a horse), symbolizing clarity, warmth, and unguarded happiness. The card signals genuine positivity, achievement, good health, and a phase of life illuminated by confidence and truth.

Reversed Reversed, The Sun can indicate temporary sadness, a delay in success, or an overly inflated ego. It often points to a dimming, rather than disappearance, of the card's natural optimism.

Judgement represents reckoning, rebirth, and answering an inner call to a higher purpose. It depicts figures rising from graves at the sound of an angel's trumpet, symbolizing awakening, self-evaluation, and absolution. The card signals a pivotal moment of reflection on past actions, culminating in renewal, forgiveness, or a decisive turning point.

Reversed Reversed, Judgement suggests self-doubt, harsh self-criticism, or failing to heed an inner calling. It can indicate avoidance of necessary reflection or difficulty forgiving oneself or others.

The World

completionfulfillmentwholenessaccomplishmentintegration

The World represents completion, fulfillment, and the successful integration of a long journey's lessons. It depicts a dancing figure encircled by a laurel wreath, surrounded by the four fixed-sign symbols, signaling wholeness and accomplishment. The card marks the end of the Fool's Journey through the Major Arcana: a cycle brought to satisfying closure, ready to begin anew at a higher level.

Reversed Reversed, The World suggests incompletion, delays, or a sense of something left unresolved. It can indicate a need for closure or difficulty moving on to the next stage of life.

The Elder Futhark

the twenty-four runes

The 24 runes of the Elder Futhark are drawn from a pouch or cast onto a cloth and read either singly or in a small spread. Most runes can land upright or inverted (merkstave/reversed), which traditionally darkens, blocks, or delays the upright sense; a handful of runes (Gebo, Hagalaz, Isa, Jera, Ingwaz, Dagaz, and sometimes Sowilo) are symmetrical or treated as non-reversible and are read the same way regardless of orientation. This Codex records the upright divinatory meaning for each rune; reversed nuance is a matter for the interpretive layer, not this reference entry.

Fehu

F · cattle / wealth

wealthabundanceearned rewardnew energymaterial security

Fehu represents cattle, the ancient Germanic measure of movable wealth, and signals material gain, prosperity, and resources gained through effort. It marks a time of abundance, success, or the arrival of new energy and opportunity. Because wealth in herds must be tended and can be lost, Fehu also carries a reminder that gains must be maintained and used wisely rather than hoarded.

Uruz

U · aurochs / wild ox

strengthvitalityraw powerenduranceuntamed will

Uruz is named for the aurochs, a powerful and now-extinct wild ox, and represents raw physical strength, vitality, and untamed will. It speaks to endurance, good health, and the courage to face challenges head-on. The rune signals a surge of primal energy and the toughening that comes through testing and hardship.

Thurisaz

TH · giant / thorn

conflictdangerreactive forceprotectioncatalyst

Thurisaz means thorn or giant, referencing the jötnar, and carries a charge of danger, conflict, and disruptive force. It can indicate a defensive or reactive power, such as a thorn that wounds or a barrier that protects, and often points to a difficult confrontation or a jolt that breaks a stagnant situation. The rune warns of impulsive or destructive energy that must be handled with caution.

Ansuz

A · a god (Odin) / divine breath

communicationwisdominspirationdivine messagespeech

Ansuz is associated with Odin and the breath or voice of the divine, representing communication, wisdom, and inspired speech. It signals messages, insight, or guidance received from a wise source, and favors learning, teaching, and persuasive or truthful communication. The rune points to the power of words and the arrival of clarifying knowledge.

Raidho

R · riding / wagon

journeymovementtravellife pathright action

Raidho means riding or the wheeled wagon, and represents travel, journeys, and forward movement in life. It can indicate a literal trip or a more figurative progression along one's path, with emphasis on rightful order, timing, and the rhythm of a well-directed course. The rune counsels steady, correct action to reach one's destination.

Kenaz

K · torch / beacon

illuminationknowledgecreativityrevealingcontrolled fire

Kenaz means torch or fire used as a tool, symbolizing controlled illumination, knowledge, and creative skill. It signals insight, learning, and the light that reveals what was hidden, as well as inspiration applied to craft or art. The rune reflects transformation through the guided use of fire, skill, and inner vision rather than destructive flame.

Gebo

G · gift / exchange

giftgenerositypartnershipreciprocitybalance

Gebo means gift, representing generosity, exchange, and the balanced reciprocity between giver and receiver. It signals partnerships, contracts, and relationships built on fair give-and-take, including gifts of love, hospitality, or sacrifice. The rune is shaped as a symmetrical X and is traditionally treated as non-reversible, carrying no inverted meaning.

Wunjo

W/V · joy

joyharmonyfellowshipfulfillmenthappiness

Wunjo means joy and represents harmony, contentment, and the pleasure of shared fellowship. It signals a period of happiness, success, and smooth cooperation with others, often after hardship has passed. The rune reflects emotional well-being and the comfort of belonging to a community or kinship group.

Hagalaz

H · hail

disruptionnatural forcesudden changetestinguncontrolled event

Hagalaz means hail, the destructive ice that falls from the sky and then melts away, symbolizing sudden disruption and uncontrollable natural force. It represents crisis, hardship, or upheaval that is beyond one's control but ultimately temporary and often clears the way for renewal. The rune is treated as non-reversible, since hail's disruptive nature is constant regardless of orientation.

Nauthiz

N · need / necessity

needconstrainthardshipresiliencenecessity

Nauthiz means need and represents constraint, hardship, and the friction of necessity. It signals resistance, delay, or want, but also the resilience and resourcefulness that emerge from being tested by difficult circumstance. The rune counsels patience and self-reliance in the face of restriction.

Isa

I · ice

stillnessstagnationpauseclarityfrozen state

Isa means ice, symbolizing stillness, stasis, and a freeze on activity or progress. It signals a period where action is blocked or must be suspended, calling for patience, caution, and clear-headed waiting rather than forcing movement. As a single unbroken vertical stroke, Isa is treated as non-reversible.

Jera

J/Y · year / harvest

harvestcyclerewardfruitionnatural timing

Jera means year or harvest, representing the natural cycle of sowing and reaping and the reward that comes from sustained effort. It signals fruition, positive change, and the fair completion of a cycle in its proper season. The rune reflects the working of natural law and is treated as non-reversible due to its symmetrical form.

Eihwaz

EI · yew tree

endurancedeath and rebirthdefensestabilitytransformation

Eihwaz is linked to the yew tree, a long-lived and poisonous tree associated with both death and endurance, and represents resilience, protection, and steady transformation. It signals a dependable foundation, defense against hardship, and the capacity to endure through change or ending. The rune reflects the link between mortality and continuity, marking transitions that strengthen rather than destroy.

Perthro

P · dice-cup / lot-container

fatemysterychancehidden thingsdivination

Perthro's exact meaning is debated but is traditionally linked to a dice-cup or container used for casting lots, representing chance, fate, and hidden or unknown outcomes. It signals mysteries, secrets, and matters governed by luck or destiny rather than direct control. The rune is associated with divination itself and with the revealing of what is presently concealed.

Algiz

Z · elk / protection

protectiondefensehigher connectionvigilanceshielding

Algiz, shaped like an outstretched hand or elk's antlers, represents protection, defense, and a shielding or warding force. It signals guardianship, vigilance against danger, and connection to higher or spiritual guidance that watches over the individual. The rune counsels alertness and invokes safety in times of vulnerability.

Sowilo

S · sun

successvitalityguidancewholenessvictory

Sowilo means sun and represents success, vitality, and life-giving energy. It signals victory, good health, and clear guidance, much as the sun lights the way and sustains life. The rune reflects wholeness, achievement, and positive momentum reaching its goal.

Tiwaz

T · the god Tyr / sky-god

justicehonorsacrificeleadershipvictory through discipline

Tiwaz is named for Tyr, the god of law and justice who sacrificed his hand to bind the wolf Fenrir, and represents honor, justice, and disciplined self-sacrifice. It signals fair victory earned through duty, courage, and adherence to principle rather than force alone. The rune favors leadership, right conduct, and standing firm for what is just.

Berkano

B · birch

growthfertilitynew beginningsnurturingrenewal

Berkano is named for the birch tree, one of the first to grow leaves in spring, and represents fertility, growth, and new beginnings. It signals nurturing energy, family matters, birth, and the careful tending needed for something new to flourish. The rune reflects the feminine, protective, and regenerative forces of nature.

Ehwaz

E · horse

partnershipprogresstrustteamworksteady movement

Ehwaz means horse, symbolizing the close working partnership between horse and rider and, by extension, trust, teamwork, and cooperative progress. It signals beneficial movement forward, loyal relationships, and steady advancement made possible through mutual reliance. The rune favors harmony between partners working toward a shared goal.

Mannaz

M · man / humankind

selfhumanitysocial orderidentitycooperation

Mannaz means man or humankind, representing the self in relation to others and the shared nature of human society. It signals self-awareness, intelligence, and the social bonds, structures, and cooperation that support communal life. The rune reflects one's place within the human order and the balance between individual identity and collective belonging.

Laguz

L · water / lake

intuitionemotionthe unconsciousflowpsychic sense

Laguz means water or lake, representing intuition, emotion, and the fluid, unconscious currents of life. It signals psychic sensitivity, imagination, and the need to flow with circumstances rather than resist them. The rune reflects the deep, sometimes hidden emotional undercurrents that guide instinct and inner life.

Ingwaz

NG · the god Ing (Freyr)

fertilitygestationpotentialcompletioninternal growth

Ingwaz is named for Ing, an early form of the god Freyr associated with fertility and peace, and represents gestation, stored potential, and internal growth building toward release. It signals a phase of quiet preparation or incubation before a goal reaches completion and manifests outwardly. The rune's symmetrical diamond form is treated as non-reversible.

Dagaz

D · day / dawn

breakthroughawakeningtransformationclaritynew dawn

Dagaz means day, representing the breakthrough of dawn light and a decisive, positive transformation. It signals awakening, clarity, and a turning point where a new and better phase begins, often bringing sudden and welcome change. The rune's symmetrical form is treated as non-reversible, reflecting its unambiguous, wholly positive charge.

Othala

O · ancestral property / homeland

heritagehometraditioninheritancebelonging

Othala means ancestral property or homeland, representing inherited wealth, family legacy, and one's roots or homeland. It signals the values, traditions, and belongings passed down through generations, along with a sense of stable belonging and identity tied to home and kin. The rune favors matters of inheritance, land, and the enduring foundation provided by one's heritage.

The Eight Trigrams

the building blocks

The eight trigrams (bagua) are the three-line building blocks of the I Ching. Each hexagram is formed by stacking two trigrams — a lower (inner) and an upper (outer) trigram. Each trigram carries a natural image, a family role (father, mother, and three sons and three daughters, ranked by which line differs from the parent trigram), and a core attribute that colors any hexagram it appears in.

☰ Qián · Heaven

The Creative · yang · the father · attribute: strong

heavencreativitystrengthinitiativethe father

Qian is composed of three unbroken lines, pure yang. Its image is Heaven, and its attribute is strength without pause — untiring, persistent movement. In the family it is the father, the originating, generative power. Wherever it appears it drives a hexagram's energy outward and upward, initiating rather than responding.

☷ Kūn · Earth

The Receptive · yin · the mother · attribute: devoted, yielding

earthreceptivitydevotionnourishmentthe mother

Kun is composed of three broken lines, pure yin. Its image is Earth, which carries and nourishes all things without competing with them. In the family it is the mother, the complementary power to Qian: where Heaven initiates, Earth completes and sustains. Its attribute is devotion — yielding strength that supports through receiving rather than forcing.

☳ Zhèn · Thunder

The Arousing · yang below two yin · the eldest son · attribute: movement, shock

thundershockmovementinitiativeeldest son

Zhen has one strong line below two yielding lines, the yang pushing up from beneath. Its image is Thunder — sudden movement that startles and arouses. In the family it is the eldest son, the first to inherit the father's initiating power. Its attribute is shock followed by movement: it breaks stillness and sets things going, often bringing initial alarm that clears into action.

☵ Kǎn · Water

The Abysmal · yang between two yin · the middle son · attribute: danger, depth

waterdangerdepthflowmiddle son

Kan has one strong line enclosed between two yielding lines, image of water held within a hollow. Its image is Water — specifically water in a ravine or repeated peril, flowing constantly toward its goal despite obstacles. In the family it is the middle son. Its attribute is danger: a hexagram touched by Kan carries genuine risk, but also water's persistence in always finding its course.

☶ Gèn · Mountain

Keeping Still · yang above two yin · the youngest son · attribute: stillness, rest

mountainstillnessrestboundaryyoungest son

Gen has one strong line resting atop two yielding lines, image of a solid cap that halts what lies beneath. Its image is Mountain — motionless, a boundary marking where movement stops. In the family it is the youngest son. Its attribute is stillness: knowing when to stop, holding firm, and providing the rest that gives later movement its meaning.

☴ Xùn · Wind/Wood

The Gentle · yin below two yang · the eldest daughter · attribute: penetration, gradual influence

windwoodgentlenesspenetrationeldest daughter

Xun has one yielding line below two strong lines, the yin entering from beneath. Its image is Wind (also Wood) — invisible, penetrating, and unable to be blocked outright, working its way gradually into every gap. In the family it is the eldest daughter. Its attribute is gentle penetration: influence that succeeds not by force but by patient, continuous, adaptable entry.

☲ Lí · Fire

The Clinging · yin between two yang · the middle daughter · attribute: clarity, dependence

firelightclarityclingingmiddle daughter

Li has one yielding line enclosed between two strong lines, image of flame that clings to fuel in order to burn. Its image is Fire, and also the Sun — brightness, clarity, and illumination. In the family it is the middle daughter. Its attribute is clinging: like fire it has no independent substance of its own but lights up whatever it attaches to, representing intelligence, awareness, and dependence on something to illuminate.

☱ Duì · Lake

The Joyous · yin above two yang · the youngest daughter · attribute: joy, openness

lakejoyopennesspleasureyoungest daughter

Dui has one yielding line resting atop two strong lines, the open yin surface above firm yang beneath. Its image is Lake — a still, open body of water that reflects and gives nourishment, and also the mouth, associated with speech and pleasure. In the family it is the youngest daughter. Its attribute is joy: cheerful, satisfying, sociable expression that arises when inner firmness meets an open, yielding surface.

The I Ching

the sixty-four hexagrams

The 64 hexagrams in the King Wen sequence, Wilhelm-Baynes names. Each is two trigrams stacked (upper over lower). A reading casts one hexagram; moving lines can turn it into a second, becoming-hexagram. The Codex gives the primary sense of each figure.

1 · The Creative (Qián)

Heaven over Heaven

strengthinitiativecreative powerperseveranceleadership

Pure yang energy, doubled: unyielding strength and tireless creative force. It counsels sustained effort, firm perseverance, and the initiative to begin great undertakings. Success comes through steady, disciplined application of strength, not through force alone. The superior person is urged to model themselves on heaven's ceaseless motion, strengthening themselves without interruption.

2 · The Receptive (Kūn)

Earth over Earth

yieldingdevotionreceptivitynurturesupport

Pure yin energy, doubled: complete receptivity, devotion, and the power to sustain and nourish. Where the Creative initiates, the Receptive completes and carries through, gaining its strength through yielding rather than resisting. It counsels following rather than leading, gentleness, and quiet strength like the earth that bears all things. Advantage comes from finding the right guidance and remaining steadfast in support of it.

3 · Difficulty at the Beginning (Zhūn)

Water over Thunder

initial chaosstrugglenew growthperseverancegathering help

A young plant pushing through hard earth: the confusion and difficulty that accompany any true beginning. Energy is present but not yet organized, so obstacles abound and the way forward is unclear. It counsels not forcing a resolution alone but seeking capable helpers, remaining firm, and consolidating small gains rather than rushing. Great progress is possible, but only through patient effort at the outset.

4 · Youthful Folly (Méng)

Mountain over Water

inexperienceinstructionlearningclaritydiscipline

A spring emerging at the foot of a mountain, not yet finding its course: the state of inexperience and the need for instruction. Success comes not from the teacher seeking the student, but from the student's sincere desire to learn; asking repeatedly or disrespectfully clouds the answer. It counsels patient, firm, and timely instruction, and a willingness to remain teachable rather than reckless.

5 · Waiting (Xū)

Water over Heaven

patiencenourishmenttimingconfidencerestraint

Clouds gathered in the sky, rain not yet fallen: a time when strength is present but the moment for action has not yet arrived. It counsels calm confidence, using the delay to nourish body and spirit rather than forcing events. Danger lies ahead, but one who waits in sincerity and does not act rashly will cross through safely when the time comes.

6 · Conflict (Sòng)

Heaven over Water

disputeoppositioncautionmediationwithdrawal

Heaven moving upward and water flowing downward, two forces working in opposing directions: the image of contention. It warns that even a just cause pursued through conflict brings danger, and that pushing a dispute to the end brings misfortune. The counsel is to seek mediation, to be willing to stop halfway, and to avoid entanglement in prolonged struggle even when one holds the right.

7 · The Army (Shī)

Earth over Water

disciplineorganizationleadershipcollective effortrestraint

Water gathered beneath the earth, hidden masses held in reserve: the image of a disciplined army led by a firm, experienced commander. Great undertakings involving many people require unified command, clear discipline, and a just cause to succeed. It counsels that strength must be organized and restrained by rule of law, used only when necessary, and directed by a mature leader who bears responsibility for those under them.

8 · Holding Together (Bǐ)

Water over Earth

unionalliancetrusttimelinessvoluntary bonding

Water resting on the earth, flowing to fill every low place without being forced: the image of natural union and mutual support. It counsels seeking closeness and alliance with others based on inner sincerity, and doing so promptly, since those who hesitate too long lose the chance to join. Union must have a worthy center to gather around, or the joining together is empty and brings misfortune.

9 · The Taming Power of the Small (Xiǎo Chù)

Wind over Heaven

restraintgentle influencegradual progresspatiencesmall means

A gentle wind moving across the sky, restraining great force through gentle, persistent influence rather than direct confrontation. Only small measures of restraint are possible now; the situation calls for patience and refinement of inner character rather than dramatic action. It counsels accumulating small gains through gentleness, since the time is not yet ripe for decisive action or open advance.

10 · Treading (Lǚ)

Heaven over Lake

conductcareful actioncourtesydanger navigatedcorrect behavior

A lake beneath the vast sky, the image of the small and yielding treading carefully behind the strong: correct conduct in a precarious situation. Like treading on a tiger's tail, one proceeds through danger safely by remaining courteous, mindful, and correct in behavior rather than provoking what is powerful. Good fortune comes to those whose conduct is cheerful and unassuming despite the risk around them.

11 · Peace (Tài)

Earth over Heaven

harmonyflourishingexchangebalanceprosperity

Heaven's energy descending and earth's energy rising to meet it: a true and fruitful exchange between forces, producing harmony and growth. It represents a high point of prosperity, when leaders and followers, or heaven and earth, are in accord and small things depart while great things approach. Even in this favorable time, it counsels remembering that peace can turn to stagnation, and urges continued care to preserve what has been achieved.

12 · Standstill [Stagnation] (Pǐ)

Heaven over Earth

stagnationobstructionwithdrawalseparationenduring hardship

Heaven rising away and earth sinking further down, forces moving apart rather than meeting: a time of blockage, misunderstanding, and stagnation between high and low. Communication fails and the superior person's way is obstructed, so it counsels withdrawing from public engagement, conserving strength, and not associating openly with those unfit for the time. Perseverance through this barren period prepares the ground for its eventual turn.

13 · Fellowship with Men (Tóng Rén)

Heaven over Fire

communityshared purposeopennessclear principlecooperation

Fire rising to join with heaven's light, the image of clarity joined with strength in genuine fellowship. True community is formed not through private cliques or narrow factions but through open-hearted union around a clear, impartial principle that all can share. It counsels seeking fellowship in the wide field rather than the inner chamber, so that cooperation is honest and free of hidden agendas.

14 · Possession in Great Measure (Dà Yǒu)

Fire over Heaven

abundancegreat wealthhumilitystewardshipclarity

The sun blazing in the height of heaven, illuminating all things: an image of great abundance and possession held at its peak. Such supreme power and wealth call for humility, generosity, and clear discernment so that abundance is used to benefit others rather than corrupt the holder. Sustained good fortune depends on restraining pride and keeping harmony with what is right despite great strength.

15 · Modesty (Qiān)

Earth over Mountain

humilitybalancequiet virtuemoderationreserve

A great mountain concealed beneath the earth, strength that does not flaunt itself: the image of true modesty, which is genuine strength held in reserve. This hexagram is unusual in being favorable in every one of its lines, since modesty rightly practiced brings success in all circumstances. It counsels leveling what is too high and raising what is too low, maintaining equilibrium through quiet, unassuming virtue rather than display.

16 · Enthusiasm (Yù)

Thunder over Earth

inspirationharmonypreparationwilling devotionmomentum

Thunder bursting forth from the earth, stirring movement and joyful energy: the image of enthusiasm that arises naturally and moves others to willing devotion. When leadership acts in accord with what is right and natural, others follow readily and without compulsion, much as movement conforms effortlessly to natural law. It counsels using this uplifting energy to prepare and organize — for defense, for music, for shared purpose — while staying alert that enthusiasm does not slide into excess or delusion.

17 · Following (Suí)

Lake over Thunder

adaptationinfluencetimingletting go to lead

The strong movement of thunder yields and settles beneath the joyous lake, teaching that true leadership means adapting to the needs of the time and the people one leads. To gain willing followers, one must first be willing to follow what is right. Success comes through flexibility, sincerity, and knowing when to accommodate rather than force.

18 · Work on What Has Been Spoiled (Gǔ)

Mountain over Wind

decayrepaircorrectionrenewal through effort

Stagnation and complacency have allowed corruption or neglect to set in, symbolized by wind trapped and rotting under a mountain. The figure calls for decisive work to repair what has decayed, requiring examination of past mistakes before new order can be restored. It counsels careful deliberation before acting, since remedying long-standing trouble demands patience.

19 · Approach (Lín)

Earth over Lake

advanceoversightgrowing influencesupervision

Two strong lines advance from below beneath four yielding lines, depicting a time of increasing influence and favorable growth, like the approach of spring. Those in authority are advised to draw near to their people with care and encouragement rather than force. The figure warns that prosperity brings responsibility, and that what advances will also, in time, recede.

20 · Contemplation (Guān)

Wind over Earth

observationreflectionexampleinsight

Wind moving over the earth reaches everywhere, symbolizing the quiet, far-reaching power of observation and contemplation. A ruler or person of standing influences others not through force but by the example of their own inner cultivation, viewed and absorbed as wind touches every blade of grass. It counsels stepping back to see one's situation clearly before acting.

21 · Biting Through (Shì Kè)

Fire over Thunder

decisive justiceremoving obstructionclarityfirm action

Something intervenes between the upper and lower jaws, like an obstacle blocking union or order, and it must be bitten through decisively. The figure calls for clear-eyed, firm action against obstruction, dishonesty, or injustice, with the understanding that force must be paired with clarity, as fire illuminates what thunder's shock must strike. Success comes to those who confront the problem directly rather than avoid it.

22 · Grace (Bì)

Mountain over Fire

adornmentformbeautysurface and substance

Fire's light plays beneath the still mountain, giving beautiful form and clarity to what is solid, symbolizing grace, adornment, and the refinement of outward appearance. The figure favors small, pleasing matters and social forms but warns that grace is a beautifying addition, not a substitute for substance. Genuine progress requires that inner content not be sacrificed for outer polish.

23 · Splitting Apart (Bō)

Mountain over Earth

declineerosioncollapse from belowwaiting out ruin

Five yielding lines rise beneath a single strong line at the top, like a mountain whose base is crumbling into the earth, depicting a time of deterioration when inferior forces are overtaking the good. The figure counsels against futile resistance and instead urges retreat, patience, and careful timing until the situation turns. It reassures that decay, once complete, prepares the ground for renewal.

24 · Return (The Turning Point) (Fù)

Earth over Thunder

renewalturning pointcyclical returnnew beginning

A single strong line returns at the bottom beneath five yielding lines, like the winter solstice when light begins its quiet return after the deepest dark. The figure marks a turning point after decline, where the way back to what is right opens naturally and without strain. It counsels rest and consolidation now, since the new growth is fragile and must not be forced.

25 · Innocence (The Unexpected) (Wú Wàng)

Heaven over Thunder

spontaneityintegritynatural correctnessunforced action

Thunder moves freely under heaven, symbolizing action that is spontaneous, sincere, and free of ulterior motive. Great success comes to one who acts in accord with their innate, uncalculated nature rather than through scheming or self-interest. The figure warns that any deviation from what is correct, however small, invites misfortune, since innocence cannot be feigned.

26 · The Taming Power of the Great (Dà Xù)

Mountain over Heaven

restraintaccumulated strengthdisciplinestored potential

The mountain holds heaven's creative power within it, symbolizing great strength that is restrained, cultivated, and stored rather than spent at once. The figure favors firm containment of energy through discipline and the study of past experience, building capacity for a great undertaking. What is held back now gathers force for a crossing of great waters later.

27 · The Corners of the Mouth (Providing Nourishment) (Yí)

Mountain over Thunder

nourishmentself-cultivationwhat one takes insustenance

The figure's open shape resembles a mouth, and its lines depict the movement of jaws, symbolizing the giving and receiving of nourishment in food, words, and ideas. It counsels careful attention to what one takes in and puts out, since both physical sustenance and speech shape one's character. True nourishment of others begins with rightly nourishing oneself.

28 · Preponderance of the Great (Dà Guò)

Lake over Wind

excessoverloadcritical massextraordinary measures

A ridgepole sags under too great a load, four strong lines crowded between weak lines at top and bottom, depicting a situation of extraordinary strain that exceeds normal capacity. The figure signals a critical, exceptional time demanding equally exceptional and sometimes solitary action to prevent collapse. It counsels careful, resolute handling of an overloaded situation before it breaks.

29 · The Abysmal (Water) (Kǎn)

Water over Water

dangerrepetitionflowing throughhabituation to risk

Water doubled falls into every hollow it meets yet keeps flowing onward, symbolizing repeated danger and the discipline needed to move through it without losing one's course. The figure counsels facing peril with sincerity, staying true to one's inner nature the way water stays true to its own level. Danger mastered through consistent, honest effort becomes a habit that carries one safely through.

30 · The Clinging, Fire (Lí)

Fire over Fire

claritybrightnessdependenceillumination

Fire has no independent form of its own; it clings to what it burns, doubled here to signal brightness that depends on something to attach to for continuance. The figure calls for cultivating clarity of mind and clinging to what is correct and nourishing, as flame clings to fuel. Sustained brilliance requires steady maintenance, not a single bright flare.

31 · Influence (Wooing) (Xián)

Lake over Mountain

attractionmutual responsecourtshipreceptivity

The lake rests above the still mountain, and the weaker element sits above the strong, depicting mutual attraction and influence exchanged freely between two open, receptive parties. The figure favors success in relationships and undertakings built on genuine, unforced responsiveness rather than domination. It marks the beginning of a new cycle of union, following naturally after the hexagrams of heaven and earth's foundational order.

32 · Duration (Héng)

Thunder over Wind

constancyendurancesteadfastnesslasting union

Thunder and wind reinforce each other in continuous, regular motion, symbolizing duration and the capacity to endure by adapting within a stable, unchanging purpose. Unlike static permanence, true duration comes from consistent movement along a fixed course, as a marriage endures through changing seasons of life. The figure counsels perseverance in one's proper direction rather than clinging rigidly to any single circumstance.

33 · Retreat (Dùn)

Heaven over Mountain

withdrawalstrategic retreattimingdignitydisengagement

The mountain rises toward heaven but heaven withdraws before it, an image of orderly retreat while strength is still intact. Retreat here is not defeat but wisdom: the superior person disengages from a worsening situation early and with dignity, rather than clinging until forced out. Small-mindedness is advancing, so the wise course is to yield ground calmly, preserving strength for a better time.

34 · The Power of the Great (Dà Zhuàng)

Thunder over Heaven

great strengthrightnessself-restraintmomentumdiscipline

Thunder sounds above heaven, a picture of great power in vigorous motion. Genuine strength must be paired with correctness and restraint, for power used without regard for what is right soon exhausts or destroys itself. The superior person does not tread on paths that are not proper, using strength only in accord with what is right.

35 · Progress (Jìn)

Fire over Earth

advancementrecognitionbrightnessrisingfavor

The sun rises over the earth, an image of rapid, illuminating advance. Unlike the omen systems catalogued elsewhere in the Codex, this bank is native to the Blue Room — its own working parts rather than borrowed symbols: the panels that read a frame, the surfaces that hold its material, and the plates that file its record. Every entry here reads the artifact — the photograph, the print, the edition, the card — and never the person who stands in it, and no entry ranks one card, or one person, above another.

Metrics is one of the three tabs on the left panel, a register reel of Diagnostic Receipts turned one plate at a time. Each plate reads the frame as a whole — Frame Signature as a silhouette rather than a score, Signal Mix as a proportion of the whole, Composition Field as a drift node set against tier bands, Frame Event as the recorded act with its bands. It is an interpretive read of the artifact's own material and may carry the material's accent colour; it is never a measure of any person and never resolves to a number on a scale.

The Diagram is the silver-instrument figure: the photograph laid on a scan sheet beneath measured overlays, always annotated. Grid, mass, focal point, gesture line, axis, arrows, and light are drawn across the frame and named with labels. It is rendered in silver rather than the card's material, and it reads the geometry of the frame — where weight falls and how the eye is led — not the figure standing inside it.
