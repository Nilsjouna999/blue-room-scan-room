# Arcana Room — Art Direction & Accuracy Pass (V1)

> Audit of the moonlit chamber plate (witch left / forge right / crown on a central dais under a moon-window ringed by zodiac dials). Merges a frame-by-frame read of the image with an 8-domain accuracy pass (blacksmithing · goldsmithing · divination · scriptorium · workshop QoL · architecture · composition · motion). Hand this to whoever draws the next version.

---

## 0. Verdict

The moon window is the best decision in the plate, and for a structural reason beyond mood: **it makes the halo architecture instead of an effect.** The previous ceremony had a halo that read as a drawn glow bolted on at the finale. Here the halo is already in the set, so consecration can be achieved by *revealing* rather than *adding* — which is the law the reading-page direction is built on. Keep it and protect it.

**The one true break: there are two different character canons in one frame.** The witch is chibi (~3–4 heads, oversized hat, small body); the smith is semi-realistic (~4.5–5 heads, adult facial structure, detailed hands). They do not live in the same world. Everything else in this document is tuning — this one is a rule. Pick one canon and draw both to a shared height chart before anything else is touched.

## 1. Proportion & scale

| # | Issue | Fix |

| 2 | **Crown is under-scaled for a hero object** — pedestal top is ~3× the crown's width, so it reads as an ornament on a table | Narrow the plinth to roughly the crown's own footprint; **raise the crown so it overlaps the lower third of the moon disc** — then the moon *is* its halo, for free |

## 2. Cut list (sleeker, in priority order)

1. **Outermost zodiac ring** → delete or reduce to a hairline. Four rings is one too many; let the moon breathe.
2. **Floor detail** → this is the highest-leverage declutter in the whole plate. Convert the floor to a **density gradient**: near-featureless dark flagstone in the mid-ground and around the dais, with detail confined to two tight clusters (worktable triangle, anvil triangle). Uniform cobble/crack texture across a large low-information area is the classic "busy but boring" failure — it adds noise without story and flattens the gradient the eye needs to travel inward. Let **a single soft pool of moonlight on the dais steps** do the work.
3. **Candles** → cut to ~3–4 purposeful sources. A dozen equal small flames is a field of micro focal points the eye can't prioritise. Fewer and larger beats many and equal.
4. **Hanging chains, upper left** → cut. Noise without meaning.
5. **Small crates / boxes / loose scatter props** → cut ~40% and **group the survivors**. Grouped tools read *quieter and more authentic* than evenly scattered ones — real workshops cluster by task.
6. **The crown on the banner, upper right** → dim hard, crop out, or change the device (anvil-and-star, or the witch's seal). This is the same duplicate-crown trap the V3.1 film audit caught; a second crown image dilutes the one being made. **Rule: exactly one crown in frame.**
7. **Wall gold linework** → restrict to structural framing (ribs, arch springers, window tracery, dais edge). Broad wall stone stays genuinely near-black. Gilded *structure*, not all-over pattern.
8. **Independent glows** → the astrolabe rings, bookshelves and tool racks must *catch* light from the moon and forge, never emit their own.
9. **Bookshelves** → background storage should be a dark textured mass, not a second reading zone. One legible open book (the ephemeris) is worth more than a wall of rendered spines.

**Rest areas to protect:** the wall zones flanking the moon window, and a clear ring of floor around the dais. In a dense symmetrical frame the eye needs unbroken dark to recover — that's what makes density read as *rich* rather than *busy*.

## 3. The six things missing that matter most

1. **A goldsmith's bench.** The single biggest accuracy unlock. **A crown is goldsmith's work, not blacksmith's** — most of it (truing, filing, chasing, stone-setting, polishing) happens at a bench, not on a great anvil. Add a bench with a **bench pin** and a **leather catch-skin slung underneath to catch gold filings**, plus mandrels and chasing hammers. This makes the story *true* rather than approximate.
2. **A forge hood and flue.** An open fire under a stone vault with nowhere for smoke to go is the #1 believability break — the ceiling would blacken evenly and the gold rings would soot up. A corbelled stone hood narrowing into a flue in the wall thickness. Bonus: its underside becomes the best soot-and-patina storytelling surface in the room.
3. **A spark screen / baffle between forge and library.** An open coal fire sharing air with unbound parchment, wax letters and a full library is a genuine hazard no smith would tolerate. Fixing it also hands you a free, elegant **compositional divider** between the two halves that still opens toward the dais.
4. **A door.** There is no way into this room. An arched threshold with a worn step, in an end wall, sized to deliver coal and vellum. A sealed chamber reads as a diorama.
5. **The raven's leg message-capsule.** The cheapest, highest-leverage story fix in the plate: right now the beat *witch seals letter → raven carries it → smith forges* has **no physical hinge**. A small clasped tube either strapped to the bird's leg or hanging on a peg by the perch makes the room's whole narrative legible at a glance.
6. **An air source for the fire** — bellows or a crank blower feeding a visible **tuyere** at the base of the firepot. Fire without a mechanism feeding it air is a bonfire, not a forge.

## 4. Prop & tool checklist by station

### 4a. Forge (blacksmith)
**Must:** firepot/hearth with visible **tuyere** · **bellows or crank blower** feeding it · **hood + flue** · **anvil on an end-grain stump** at hip height, horn pointing *away* from the fire into the swing arc · **hardy tools** seated in the hardy hole · **tongs in several jaw types on a rack** at the fire (grabbed by feel, not searched for) · proper smithing hammer (cross/straight-peen, 1.5–3 lb — *not* a warhammer) · **fire tools: rake, poker, slice** · **slack tub** deep enough to submerge the work · **leg/post vise on its own floor post** (not a bench vise) · coal/coke store + visible ash and clinker.
**Nice:** swage block · **cone mandrel** (directly apt — a crown is a ring form) · flux dish · punches/drifts/chisels · wire scale-brush.
**Keep the working triangle tight:** fire → anvil → slack tub within 2–3 steps. Big gaps read as staged.
**Sparks must originate at the hammer/workpiece contact point** — never float free.

### 4b. Goldsmith's bench (new — the accuracy unlock)
**Must:** **bench pin + semicircular cutout with leather catch-skin below** · **crown/ring mandrel** · **draw plate + draw tongs** for wire · **planishing and chasing hammers** (visibly lighter and different-headed from the smith's) · **pitch bowl** for repoussé (low, wide, matte black — must *not* read as a cauldron) · gravers, burnishers, needle files · **blowpipe + spirit lamp** (pre-industrial; not a modern torch) · **crucible + tongs + ingot mould** · **assay balance and weights** (instantly signals precious-metal work) · **touchstone + testing needles** (purity — thematically perfect for a consecration) · loupe, calipers/dividers.
**Nice:** borax dish · pickle pot · rouge cloth and buff · **thin brass crown-curve templates pinned to the wall** · a carved wax fleuron master.
**Show process-evidence, not jewel-piles:** filings in the skin, scorch by the lamp, a wax fragment by the pitch bowl. A scatter of loose gems is set dressing; filings are truth.

### 4c. Divination table
**Must:** tarot on a **silk wrap** with its box, laid in a **real spread** (three-card row or Celtic cross) — never fanned like a card trick · **casting cloth with marked zones** under a **rune pouch with runes actually thrown** (runes are cast, not arranged in a tidy row) · an **ephemeris** open to dense numeric tables **+ a house-wheel star chart + dividers and straightedge** — chart-casting is navigation, and this is the combination that makes the astrology literate · a **ledger** of readings and commissions taken, open beside the wax · an **obsidian scrying mirror** (dark and reflective, not silvered).
**Nice:** yarrow stalks *or* three coins for the I Ching (pick one) · a numerology correspondence chart tucked as a bookmark · a pendulum hanging still · a small armillary sphere or celestial globe · an hourglass mid-run.
**Highest leverage here:** angle the star chart so its drawn house-wheel **visually rhymes with the rotating zodiac rings in the window above.** That one choice turns the moon window from ambient lighting into part of her practice, and makes the left half read as astrology-literate rather than generic-witchy.
**The crystal ball should be inert glass catching ambient light** — not internally lit like a lamp.

### 4d. Scriptorium — write, seal, dispatch
**Must:** **sloped desk** · quill **+ a pen-knife** for re-cutting the nib · two inkwells in a fitted stand · **pounce pot** (perforated lid) for drying wet ink before folding — skipping this is the #1 tell an artist doesn't know the process · parchment **stored rolled or flat under weights**, never loose-scattered · **page weights** · **sealing wax stick** (shellac-resin, matte — not a candle taper) **+ a dedicated small flame** to melt it **+ a sealing spoon** · **the signet/seal matrix**, kept in its own stand or on a cord — this is the object that authenticates the whole plot, so render it as slightly precious · ribbon or waxed cord for tying before the wax goes over · **the raven's leg-capsule** · **perch with a droppings board**.
**Nice:** seed and water dishes · a dish of discarded practice-seal blobs · a bone folder · a document tube for outgoing letters.
**The readable sequence is: write → pounce → fold/tie → melt → stamp → dispatch.** If a viewer can trace that chain, the room is doing its job.

### 4e. Room services & quality of life
**Must:** spark screen (§3.3) · hood and flue (§3.2) · **sand bucket + damp hide** beside the forge (water can explode on burning coal — the slack tub is for quenching metal, not for fire-fighting) · a **condenser lamp** at the worktable — a water-filled glass sphere focusing a single candle, a real period optical tool, gorgeous, and it rhymes with the crystal ball · **a stool at the witch's table and none at the anvil** (smiths stand and pivot; a seated smith is the classic tell) · **a hearth plate** of metal or slate set into the floor under the forge · **labelled storage** — a flat plan chest for star charts, and a **shadow board** (painted tool silhouettes) above the smith's rack. Shadow boards read as daily-used *and* give the eye an organising grid instead of random objects — and gold silhouettes on near-black is exactly your visual language.
**Nice:** library ladder · a small cistern/ewer to explain where quench water comes from · swarf tray and hearth broom · **a velvet cushion or cloth-lined tray under the crown** on the pedestal (presented, not dumped on bare stone).

### 4f. Architecture
**Must:** the flue (§3.2) · the door (§3.4) · a **floor drain or stone sump** by the slack tub with the floor slightly graded to it · **two distinct floor wear zones** — a scorched, spark-pocked apron before the anvil, versus a smoother zone under the scribing table showing ink and wax stains · **vault ribs that actually land** on corbels or responds (a load path, not a texture) · the moon window needs **real stone tracery/mullions and a thickened surround**, with the gold rings mounted as a separate visible armature in front of it — otherwise the rings read as a floating HUD element rather than a built object.
**Nice:** a low ash/ventilation hatch · patina gradient on the dais treads (centre more worn than edges, implying repeated ritual traffic).

## 5. Motion — fixed stage, spinning cosmos

The conceit is: **the room holds; the universe turns.** Layer it strictly.

- **Ring rotation at mutually irrational periods** (e.g. ~47s / ~76s / ~123s — near golden-ratio spacing). Never 2:1 or 3:1, or the glyphs periodically realign and the eye catches the loop.
- **Counter-rotate the glyphs so they stay upright.** On a real astrolabe the rete turns but its pointers stay radially legible — that legibility is what reads as *instrument* rather than *magic circle*. Tumbling glyphs = ornament. Go instrument; it matches the "ruled instrument sheet" canon.
- **Starfield rotates about the window's centre** at radius-dependent speeds — never a lateral scroll, which implies a camera pan and breaks the fixed stage.
- **One thin cloud band** crossing the moon, once, slowly. More reads as weather.
- **The moon is the ceremony's diegetic clock** — its position/phase progresses in step with the four beats (reading → sealing → forging → consecration), so time and ritual stage are legible without any UI.
- **The crown gets zero motion** — no shimmer, no rim-light animation, nothing, until consecration. Everything else turns or breathes; the crown's absolute stillness is what marks it as the hero and the fixed point the cosmos orbits.
- **Ambient life allowed to the room, and nothing more:** forge flicker (layered noise, never a hand-keyed cycle), **candles flickering out of phase with each other**, one steam wisp off the cup. Sparks only at hammer contact.
- **Banned:** camera drift or breathing zoom "for cinematic feel" (it undercuts the entire conceit), looping walk/hammer/flight cycles, floor-wide fog or dust, glow overlays on the crown.
- **Nice:** faint rotating shadow-lines cast by the rings onto the floor around the dais — ties the turning sky to the static room without moving any room geometry.

## 6. The brief, rewritten

Your prompt was strong on mood and weak on constraint. Add these, and the next pass won't drift:

1. **Character canon** — one stylization, stated head-count ratio, shared height chart. *Most likely thing to break.*
2. **Hero hierarchy** — "exactly one crown in frame; the crown is the only object with object-depth; the crown has the highest local contrast and sharpest edge in the image; nothing else may be gold-lit."
3. **Light motivation and count** — moon = cool key, largest; forge = warm, second; candles = tertiary connective tissue only, never a third main source. No independent glows anywhere.
4. **Negative-space budget** — name a figure (~30% of frame near-empty) and name *where* the rest areas are, or density creeps back every revision.
5. **Density gradient** — periphery detailed, mid-ground floor near-empty, dais highest contrast. State it explicitly; it is the device that makes a small object the hero of a wide frame.
6. **Static plate or animated background?** If animated, list which layers move — and state that the room does not.
7. **Safe margins** — if the reading page or any UI ever overlays this, what must stay clear.
8. **Aspect ratio, resolution, and whether it must survive a crop** to other ratios.
9. **Wear and patina, named per zone** — soot under the forge hood, scorch apron before the anvil, ink and wax stains at the desk, worn dais treads. **The most convincing "detail" comes from use, not from adding objects** — this is how you get richer *and* cleaner at the same time.
10. **A "traceable function" rule** — every visible object must be nameable as belonging to blacksmithing, goldsmithing, one of the five divination systems, scribing/dispatch, or room services. If it can't be named, it's clutter and it goes.

## 7. One-line rules to keep on the wall

- Exactly one crown in frame.
- The crown never moves and is never improved — only revealed.
- Group tools by task; never scatter them to fill space.
- Fewer, larger lights beat many equal ones.
- Detail earns its place by being *used*, not by being *added*.
- If it can't be named, it's clutter.
